**8. Why this result was reached**

The preliminary result is **can_proceed** because, based on the information provided, the complaint is framed as a challenge to the administrative judge’s **conduct during the TAL hearing**, not as a request to correct the merits of the March 14, 2025 decision.

That distinction matters. An **appeal or review** argues that the decision was wrong in law, wrong in fact, procedurally invalid, or should be changed. A party pursuing that route would usually ask for relief such as:
- reversing or modifying the decision;
- ordering a new hearing;
- reconsidering the evidence;
- correcting legal findings; or
- granting a different substantive result, such as repairs, rent reduction, or another remedy.

By contrast, an **ethics complaint** is about whether the administrative judge’s behaviour met the standards of dignity, impartiality, fairness, and proper judicial conduct expected in the hearing process. The user has expressly stated that:
- they understand the CJA is not an appeal body;
- they are not asking the CJA to change or overturn the TAL decision;
- they are not seeking damages, a fine, a new hearing, or a different judge for the original matter; and
- they want the judge’s conduct reviewed for possible disciplinary or corrective consequences.

Those stated objectives strongly support treatment as an ethics matter rather than an appeal.

The allegations also concern **specific conduct**, not just dissatisfaction with losing the case. The described issues include:
- sarcastic and dismissive comments directed at the user;
- repeated interruptions and curtailing of the user’s evidence;
- more permissive treatment of the landlord’s participation;
- an apparent prompt or legal assist given to the landlord; and
- a possible one-sided communication during a break.

If accurately supported, those allegations can reasonably be characterized as potential concerns about:
- respectful treatment of a party;
- equal treatment of the parties;
- apparent impartiality;
- improper assistance to one side; and
- possible ex parte or one-sided communication.

These are the kinds of concerns that may fall within an ethics review framework because they focus on **how the hearing was conducted** and how the judge behaved toward the parties.

At the same time, some parts of the factual background may overlap with matters that can also arise in appeals, such as interruption of evidence, rejection of proof, or reliance on certain legal arguments. On their own, those kinds of procedural rulings do not automatically become ethics violations. A judge may control a hearing firmly, limit repetitive evidence, or ask clarifying questions without misconduct. What makes this intake potentially suitable for the CJA is the additional allegation of **sarcasm, unequal treatment, apparent coaching of one side, and possible communication outside the other party’s presence**, which moves the complaint beyond ordinary disagreement with case management or the result.

So, the complaint may proceed **in principle** if it is framed properly:
- as a complaint about the judge’s words, conduct, and hearing management;
- not as an argument that the TAL reached the wrong result on heating, repairs, notice, or rent reduction; and
- not as a request for the CJA to substitute its view of the evidence or law.

If the user later wants to challenge the correctness of the March 14, 2025 TAL decision itself, that would generally require a **separate appeal, review, or other remedy**, if available, and would not be decided through the CJA ethics process.

There is enough information to classify the matter for preliminary triage. The missing details do **not** prevent classification, but they may matter for filing strength and precision. In particular, it would still help to confirm:

In short, the matter appears to fit the CJA process because the core complaint is about **judicial conduct and appearance of impartiality during the hearing**, not simply about whether the TAL decision was right or wrong.
